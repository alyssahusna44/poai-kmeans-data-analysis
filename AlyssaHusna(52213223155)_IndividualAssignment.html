3. K-Means Clustering (15 points)

3.1 Choosing appropriate value for K based on analysis and justify.

To determine optimal value of K, two methods were used:

  1. Elbow Method: analyse the inertia (sum of squared distances to cluster centers)
  2. Silhouette Score: check how well each data point fits within its cluster

Both methods will be visualized, and the results compared to select the best value for K.

3.1.1 Elbow Method

  • A technique used to identify the optimal number of clusters (K) for K-Means clustering by looking at the inertia.
  • Inertia is the sum of squared distances of each data point to its assigned cluster center. The idea is that as K increases, inertia decreases.
  • However, theres a point where inertia starts to decrease at a much slower rate, which is the elbow point. This is where adding more clusters does not significantly improve the clustering.
  • This method helps identify the best balance between underfitting(K is too small, leading to overly simplistic models that fail to capture underlying patterns in the data.) and overfitting(K is too large, creating too many clusters, some of which may be unnecessary or not meaningful.).

TL;DR The elbow point on the curve (the point before the bent part) indicates optimal K, where adding another cluster does'nt significantly reduce inertia. This method helps in finding the best balance between underfitting and overfitting.

3.1.2 Silhouette Score

  • The score ranges from -1 to 1, where a score closer to 1 indicates well-separated clusters.
  • It can only be computed for K values > 1, as K=1 doesnt make sense in terms of measuring separation between clusters. So the range will be a bit different from earlier when using elbow method.
  • It measures how similar each point is to its own cluster compared to other clusters, basically evaluates quality of clustering.
  • A higher silhouette score means better-defined clusters and optimal K maximizes this sco.

TL;DR The silhouette score measures how similar each point is to its own cluster compared to others. Higher score shows better-defined clusters. The optimal K is one with highest score.

In[739]:
# Silhouette Score to determine the optimal number of clusters
silhouette_scores = []
k_range = range(2, 11)
for k in k_range:
    kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=50)
    kmeans.fit(customer_data_scaled_df[['Age', 'Spending Score (1-100)']])
    score = silhouette_score(customer_data_scaled_df[['Age', 'Spending Score (1-100)']], kmeans.labels_)
    silhouette_scores.append(score)    

# Plot Scores
sns.lineplot(x=k_range, y=silhouette_scores, marker='o')
plt.title("Silhouette Score")
plt.xlabel("Number of Clusters (K)")
plt.ylabel("Silhouette Score")
plt.show()

Explaination:

  • The silhouette score analysis suggests that the best clustering is achieved with K=2, as this value provides the highest silhouette score, indicating that the two clusters are well-separated and cohesive.
  • This indicates that the dataset has a natural division into two distinct groups based on age and spending score.

Selecting the Optimal K

In my analysis, I first used the elbow method to determine the optimal number of clusters. However, the inertia continued to decrease smoothly without a clear bend, suggesting that the elbow method was not conclusive in this case.

Then I used the silhouette score to determine the optimal K. The silhouette score analysis showed that the number of clusters peaked at K=2, which indicates the best cohesion and separation between clusters.

Based on this analysis, the data seems to naturally separate into two distinct groups. Therefore, *K=2 is chosen as the optimal number of clusters*, as it maximizes the silhouette score and provides the best separation of customer segments.
